Case study · 2025

Tracking meals
should feel effortless.

A chat-first calorie tracker built around one idea: logging what you eat should be as easy as describing it. This is the end-to-end story of how Chewy was designed and built.

01

Discover

What's the problem?

"Most nutrition apps fail the consistency test — too many taps, too much friction for something you're supposed to do every single day."

The observation

Existing calorie trackers like MyFitnessPal require searching databases, logging each ingredient separately, and navigating multiple screens just to record a meal. The friction makes consistency feel like a chore.

The inspiration

Hevy — a gym tracking app — showed that good design can make a tedious habit feel effortless. Its clean, minimal UX became the design benchmark for Chewy: every interaction should feel obvious and fast.

Key insights

People know what they ate. They don't need a database — they need a place to describe it quickly and move on. The logger should meet them at the level of natural language, not structured data entry.

Friction kills habits Natural language is faster Simplicity drives consistency Good design is invisible
02

Define

Who are we designing for?

"How might we make logging meals so frictionless that it becomes a natural, automatic part of someone's day?"

Problem statement

People who want to track nutrition struggle to maintain the habit because existing apps demand too much effort per log. The barrier between intention and action is too high.

Target user

Health-conscious individuals who have tried calorie tracking before but abandoned it due to the effort involved. They know what they eat — they just need a faster way to record it.

Jobs to be done

Log a meal in under 10 seconds. Understand today's nutritional intake at a glance. Build and save meals I eat repeatedly. Review history to understand patterns.

Success criteria

A user can log any meal with a single text description. The app handles the macro calculation. The experience feels more like messaging a friend than filling a form.

03

Develop

How do we build it?

"Three modes, one interface — Log it, Check it, or turn it into a Recipe."

Core interaction

A chat-first input bar at the bottom of the screen. Users type a natural language description of their meal. The AI parses it and returns macros. One tap logs it. No forms, no databases, no friction.

Three modes

Log — instant log with macro return. Check — check macros before committing, tap bubble to confirm. Create — turn a description into a saved recipe with ingredients and instructions.

Design decisions

Warm paper palette instead of clinical white. Typography-led hierarchy. Progress bars instead of pie charts. Minimal tab bar with three clear destinations: Chat, Meals, Profile.

Warm paper background Avenir Next typography Pink-red accent #f00078

Iterations

Started with a dark theme → moved to warm light palette for legibility and warmth. Removed Create button from main input to reduce cognitive load. Added long-press to convert logged meals to recipes.

04

Deliver

What did we ship?

"A working end-to-end product — from chat to macros, recipes to history — built solo across product, design, and engineering."

Final product

A React Native app with three tabs: a chat-first food logger, a recipe library, and a profile with daily goals, meal history, calendar, and a 7-day chart. Backed by Supabase and a custom AI parsing function.

Technical outcomes

End-to-end authentication flow. Real-time macro tracking. AI-powered meal parsing via Supabase Edge Functions. Cross-session goal persistence. Recipe CRUD with ingredient management.

Learnings

Building end-to-end alone forces prioritisation. Every feature has a cost — in time, complexity, and UX clarity. The biggest lesson: constraint is a creative tool. Less surface area, more depth.

What's next

Photo-to-log via camera input. Audio description mode. Meal sharing between users. A more refined onboarding that communicates the chat-first model before the user's first session.
